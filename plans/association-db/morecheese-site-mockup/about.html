About

A federation of makers, since 1947.

Founded by twelve cheesemakers in a barn outside Madison, Wisconsin who could not agree on much except that someone had to write down what good cheese was. We've been arguing — productively — ever since.

Mission

To protect, advance, and steward the craft of cheesemaking — through standards, education, advocacy, and community.

We are a 501(c)(6) member-driven non-profit. We are not a trade group, not a regulator, not a marketing board. We exist for the people who make cheese, the people who sell cheese, the people who study cheese, and the people who teach cheese. The dues our members pay fund the work no individual maker can do alone.

79 years

The arc.

1947
Twelve Wisconsin cheesemakers found the American Cheese Standards Society at a barn in Cross Plains. The original mission: write down what good cheese is, because the post-war commodity boom is producing cheese that isn't.
1958
First published standards: cheddar (8 grades), Swiss-type (4 grades), American process. Adopted within 3 years by the USDA.
1971
First international chapter (Pays de la Loire, France). Name changes to International Cheese Federation. French and English co-equal languages.
1984
Master Cheesemaker certification launched. First cohort: 14 candidates, 3 years of program, 9 finishers.
1996
Co-founding member of Codex Alimentarius Cheese Working Group. Standards begin formally informing UN/FAO food code.
2008
First formal advocacy filing — FDA proposed rule on raw-milk cheese aging requirements. The federation's position adopted in final rule.
2019
Apprenticeship program launched in partnership with 60 producer-mentors. First cohort: 60 apprentices, $1.8M in stipend funding.
2024
6,000+ members across 38 countries for the first time. The European chapters now outnumber the North American.
2026
79th Annual Conference in Madison — the place it all started. Coming September 18–21.

Committees

The work gets done in committees.

Twelve standing committees, plus working groups that form and dissolve around specific topics. Any Professional-tier member or above is eligible to serve. We always need help.

Standards Committee

Writes and maintains the published standards. 23 members. Chair: Robert Kihm.

Education Committee

Owns the certifications and CE library. 18 members. Chair: Jamie Wu.

Awards Committee

Runs the National Cheese Awards. 12 members. Chair: John Huisman.

Advocacy Committee

Files regulatory positions, organizes member-mobilization. 15 members. Chair: Tom Whitaker.

Research Council

Funds and reviews research at university partners. 14 members. Vice-Chair: Sarah Henderson.

Apprenticeship Committee

Selects apprentices and pairs them with mentor producers. 10 members.

Corporate sponsors

Sponsors are visible. They are not members.

Sponsor dollars fund specific programs (Apprenticeship, Research Council, Awards). Sponsors get recognition and access to non-membership engagement (event tables, conference branding) — but never voting rights, never standards influence, never policy positions. This is non-negotiable.
