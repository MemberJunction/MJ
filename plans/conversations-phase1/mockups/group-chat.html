Group Chat

Multiple humans and one or more agents in a single conversation — roster, invites, per-user attribution, presence, and concurrent-agent indicators. Phase 1 adds the Conversation Participants table (Role: Owner/Member/Guest, Status: Invited/Active/Removed) and Conversation.IsGroup; runtime arrives in Phase 2.

What: A persistent team channel. Roster sidebar shows every human (with online/away/offline presence dots) and agents in their own group; each person owns a stable attribution color used on their avatar, name, and a left rail. @-mention works for humans and agents; an invite button drives the Invited→Active flow.

Why world-class: Borrows the muscle-memory of Slack/Teams — zero learning curve. Color-coded attribution scales to many speakers without losing track of who said what; presence + typing make the room feel live; agents sit naturally beside people as first-class participants.

Tradeoff: The roster eats horizontal space and the channel metaphor implies "always-on rooms," which is heavier than MJ's current conversation-per-task model — risk of room sprawl.

What: Humans converse on the left; every dispatched agent gets its own live lane on the right showing real-time state (Running / Queued / Done), current step, and progress. Multiple agents run truly in parallel; finished lanes post results back into the shared discussion.

Why world-class: Makes the Phase-2 concurrency story visible — you see three agents racing at once instead of a single serialized "thinking…" spinner. Dependencies (Fix Drafter waiting on Log Analyst) and parallelism are legible at a glance, which is exactly what a high-stakes triage room needs.

Tradeoff: Two-column density is demanding on small screens and for casual chats it's overkill — best reserved for power scenarios where multi-agent fan-out is the point, not the default group view.

What: A calm, document-like group. Messages spawn collapsible threads (agents reply inside the relevant thread, not the main flow). Read receipts show who's seen each message; a lightweight Invite→Accept/Decline card handles guests; each participant tunes their own notification level.

Why world-class: Threading keeps a multi-person + multi-agent conversation from collapsing into noise — the #1 failure mode of group chat. Read receipts and per-person prefs respect attention; it stays closest to MJ's existing focused, task-scoped conversation model while adding just enough group machinery.

Tradeoff: Threading adds a navigation layer (reply-here vs. reply-in-thread) some users find fiddly, and concurrent-agent activity is less dramatic here — parallel work shows as multiple typing/thread indicators rather than a dedicated dashboard.
